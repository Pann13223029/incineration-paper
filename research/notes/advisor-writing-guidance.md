# Advisor Writing Guidance

This note turns the advisor-provided writing resources into repo-specific rules for thesis revision.

Source materials reviewed:
- `research_paper_template.docx`
- `How to write a scientific paper in fifteen steps.pdf`
- `The art and science of writing a publishable article.pdf`

## Core principle

Write the thesis as a front-loaded explanatory paper:
- state the main point early
- make the narrative linear
- keep the architecture obvious
- separate reporting from interpretation
- reduce reader effort as much as possible

The thesis should not read like exploratory writing or a mystery. The reader should know within the opening paragraphs:
- what the question is
- why it matters
- what the two-part design is
- what the main answer is

## Structural rules

For submission-facing writing, keep a conventional scientific spine even if chapter labels differ:
- Abstract
- Keywords
- Introduction
- Literature Review
- Methodology
- Results
- Discussion
- Conclusion
- References
- Appendices if needed

For this thesis, the strongest narrative spine is:
1. extensive margin: observed transition into power generation
2. intensive margin: efficiency heterogeneity among generators

Everything in the manuscript should serve that two-part arc.

## Non-negotiable writing rules

1. Main point first.
The working thesis of the paper must be expressible as one short declarative sentence. Every section and paragraph should help defend or clarify that sentence.

2. Front-load the contribution.
The first two paragraphs should make the research problem, scope, and payoff explicit. Do not bury the contribution deep in the introduction.

3. Use a roadmap.
The introduction should tell the reader how the paper proceeds. Each section should also open with a local roadmap sentence.

4. Keep methods and results paired.
Every defended result should trace back to a method, and every method retained in the thesis should lead to a result that matters.

5. Keep results and discussion distinct.
Results should report what the analysis shows. Discussion should interpret what those results mean, how far they travel, and what they do not establish.

6. Keep claims narrower than evidence.
Findings may synthesize several results, but no finding should outrun the outputs. If a claim needs heavy qualification, either narrow it or move it to limitations/discussion.

7. Write simply.
Prefer short, exact sentences over long, ornamental ones. Avoid jargon when plain language is available. Use active voice where it improves clarity.

8. Make paragraphs do one job.
Each paragraph should have:
- a topic sentence
- support or evidence
- a transition or closing implication

9. Surface limitations explicitly.
List the main problem items:
- caveats
- scope limits
- objections
- identification limits
Then answer each one directly in the discussion or limitations.

10. Respect the audience.
Assume the supervisor or examiner is busy and skeptical. Make the writing easy to follow without requiring them to reconstruct the logic.

## Current thesis implications

These rules reinforce the current direction of the thesis:
- the two-part adoption/efficiency architecture is a strength and should stay central
- the title, abstract, and opening paragraphs should lead with the two-part architecture and defended main claim
- results sections should stay empirical and restrained
- policy language belongs in discussion/conclusion, not embedded in raw results reporting
- limitations should explicitly cover observed adoption, mechanism ambiguity, and non-causal scope

## Revision checklist

Before considering a major chapter or full-draft revision complete, check:

- Can the thesis be summarized in one strong declarative sentence?
- Does the introduction follow a territory -> niche -> occupation structure?
- Is the two-part narrative visible early and consistently?
- Does every major method produce a corresponding defended result?
- Are results paragraphs mostly reporting rather than interpreting?
- Does the discussion convert results into findings without overstating?
- Are the main objections named and answered directly?
- Does each paragraph have a clear topic sentence?
- Is the wording simpler after revision, not more elaborate?
- Would a busy supervisor know where to start and what matters within 2-3 pages?

## Operational use

Use this note as a style gate for:
- abstract rewrites
- introduction restructuring
- chapter compression
- discussion/conclusion tightening
- supervisor-facing packet polishing

This is a writing and presentation guide, not a license to change defended empirical claims without rerunning the evidence workflow.
