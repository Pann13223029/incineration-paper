# Defense Run Sheet

Timed delivery guide for the current defense deck. Use this with:

- `research/slides/defense-deck.md`
- `research/notes/defense-rapid-answers.md`
- `research/notes/defense-question-order.md`

The goal is to keep the talk tight enough that questions start while you still look in control.

---

## Recommended default: 8-9 minutes

### Slide 0. Title

- Time: `0:20`
- Job: one-sentence setup
- Say: `This thesis asks two different questions with two different frames: who actually transitions into power generation, and conditional on generation, what predicts performance.`

### Slide 1. Thesis in One Sentence

- Time: `0:45`
- Job: explain the redesign
- Say:
  `The earlier version asked a selected generator sample to explain the whole fleet. The redesign fixes that by separating observed entry into generation from conditional generator efficiency.`

### Slide 2. One Dataset, Two Frames

- Time: `0:55`
- Job: anchor the data and the sample split
- Say:
  `Everything comes from one administrative panel, but the extensive and intensive margins use different frames because they answer different questions.`

### Slide 3. Selective Adoption

- Time: `1:15`
- Job: deliver the first main result
- Say:
  `Observed transition into generation is selective, not diffuse. Older-than-10-year facilities are less likely to transition, and larger facilities are more likely to do so.`

### Slide 4. Pathway Audit

- Time: `1:10`
- Job: calibrate the mechanism claim
- Say:
  `The pathway audit keeps the policy section honest. It shows that reset or rebuild-like events are the largest observed bucket, but it does not prove one uniquely identified mechanism.`

### Slide 5. Conditional Efficiency

- Time: `1:20`
- Job: deliver the second main result
- Say:
  `Among generators, the biggest gaps remain across facilities rather than within the same facility over time. That is why the thesis talks about bounded responsiveness rather than easy operational reversibility.`

### Slide 6. Why Not Two-Way FE

- Time: `1:05`
- Job: preempt the hardest methods challenge
- Say:
  `Age rises mechanically with year, and only 14.99 percent of the signal is within-facility. So a ritual FE design would fit the convention better than the question.`

### Slide 7. Claims and Non-Claims

- Time: `0:55`
- Job: control overclaim risk before the committee does it for you
- Say:
  `The thesis is stronger because its scope now matches its evidence. It makes a disciplined observational claim, not a single-mechanism causal claim.`

### Slide 8. Bottom Line

- Time: `0:40`
- Job: close on contribution and policy hierarchy
- Say:
  `Operations matter, but they do not appear large enough to carry the whole transition by themselves.`

---

## Short version: 5-6 minutes

Use Slides `0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8`.

Skip:

- Slide 4 unless mechanism questions are likely
- Slide 6 unless the committee is methods-heavy
- Slide 7 unless you need explicit scope control before questions

Short close:

`The contribution is not that I prove one causal mechanism. It is that I show, with a reproducible two-part design, where transition is actually occurring and where bounded responsiveness still constrains performance.`

---

## Longer version: 10-12 minutes

Keep all main slides and add one appendix if needed:

- Appendix A for methods-heavy audiences
- Appendix B if mechanism challenge is likely
- Appendix C if the discussion is drifting into overclaim or theory language

Do not add more than one appendix before questions unless the format explicitly expects a longer lecture.

---

## Delivery rules

- Never read coefficient ranges aloud unless asked.
- Lead with the interpretation, then mention the number.
- If a question is hostile, answer with the narrow defended claim first.
- When unsure, prefer `observed transition`, `coded panel`, and `strongly consistent with` over stronger language.
- End every difficult answer by returning to what the thesis actually contributes.

## Hostile-answer template

Use this shape when a question feels aggressive:

1. `Short answer: no` or `short answer: yes, but only in a limited sense`
2. one sentence on what the evidence directly shows
3. one sentence on what it does not identify
4. one sentence returning to the calibrated thesis claim
