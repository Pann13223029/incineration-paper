# Chapter 6: Conclusion

## 6.1 Summary of Findings

This thesis set out to answer a focused empirical question: what facility characteristics predict energy recovery efficiency among Japan's power-generating incinerators, and how has this changed as the fleet modernizes? The analysis, drawing on 23,599 facility-year observations from the Ministry of the Environment's General Waste Treatment Survey (FY2005–FY2024), produces findings that are both empirically robust and theoretically consequential.

Three determinants of efficiency are established with high confidence across all eight model specifications. Facility age reduces efficiency by approximately 2.8–4.3% per year (p < 0.001 in all specifications), with mean efficiency declining monotonically from 0.397 MWh/t among facilities aged 0–10 years to 0.179 MWh/t among facilities over 30 years old. Design capacity improves efficiency by approximately 0.08–0.10 log-units per 100 additional tonnes per day (p < 0.001), reflecting the engineering economics of boiler and turbine systems at scale. Capacity utilization improves efficiency by approximately 0.53–0.83 log-units across the full utilization range (p < 0.001), reflecting thermodynamic gains from operating near design conditions.

Two variables fail to predict efficiency. Waste heating value is statistically insignificant in every specification, suggesting that waste composition management is not a reliable lever for improving energy recovery. Grid emission factors produce sign-unstable coefficients that reverse when year fixed effects are included, indicating that this variable proxies for time trends rather than a genuine causal mechanism.

The deepest finding of the study, however, is not a regression coefficient. The within-facility variation ratio of approximately 0.11 — the fact that roughly 89% of all efficiency variation in the dataset is between facilities rather than within facilities over time, a ratio stable across pre- and post-Fukushima subsamples — establishes that efficiency is design-determined at construction and does not respond meaningfully to operational intervention over a facility's life. This is the empirical signature of infrastructure lock-in.

At the fleet level, Japan's incineration sector has experienced meaningful change over the study period: facility count has fallen 23% (from 1,318 to 1,014 facilities), and the power-generating share has nearly doubled (from 21.6% to 41.1%). Gross avoided CO₂ emissions from waste-to-energy reached approximately 4.6 million t-CO₂ in FY2024 (an upper bound, as process emissions from combustion are not deducted). But 59% of active facilities still generate no electricity, and the efficiency gains of the modernizing segment are concentrated in a small number of large, modern plants — a pattern that is simultaneously encouraging (the transition is occurring) and concerning (it is occurring too slowly and unevenly for net-zero compatibility).

## 6.2 Theoretical Contributions

This thesis makes two contributions to the industrial ecology literature. First, it provides facility-level empirical support for the carbon lock-in hypothesis in the waste sector — a domain where the theoretical prediction has been discussed but rarely subjected to quantitative test. The near-zero within-facility efficiency variation, consistent across 947 facilities over up to 20 years of observation, constitutes unusually direct evidence that physical infrastructure embeds design decisions in ways that are practically irreversible through operational means. This enriches the lock-in literature by demonstrating the mechanism at work in a non-energy-generation context.

Second, it demonstrates the value of the fleet heterogeneity perspective in analyzing infrastructure transitions. Aggregate statistics on Japan's WtE sector — rising power-generating share, improving fleet average efficiency, growing avoided emissions — present a picture of progress. The heterogeneity analysis reveals that this aggregate progress coexists with a large, stagnant, and climatically costly segment of the fleet that is not improving and, on current trajectories, will persist for decades. The policy implications of these two pictures differ dramatically: aggregate-level analysis supports an incrementalist approach; heterogeneity analysis calls for targeted intervention — specifically, accelerated retirement and replacement of the oldest, smallest, and least efficient facilities.

## 6.3 Policy Recommendations

The findings support four policy recommendations for Japan's waste management sector as it navigates the path to carbon neutrality.

**First, accelerate targeted retirement of pre-1995 non-power-generating facilities.** These facilities burn waste with no energy recovery and, given their age, face replacement cycles in any case. National subsidies for early retirement — potentially tied to regional consolidation plans that route waste to modern facilities — would bring the transition timeline closer to net-zero compatibility. The data show that the efficiency benefit of replacing a 1985-vintage 80 t/day facility with a 2024-vintage 200 t/day facility is approximately twofold — a gain no operational improvement can approach.

**Second, scale regional consolidation planning from municipal to prefecture level.** The current municipality-by-municipality approach to facility planning perpetuates a fragmented structure that prevents the scale economies the regression results identify as a primary efficiency driver. Prefecture-level waste management plans that identify optimal facility locations, sizes, and inter-municipal waste routing arrangements — backed by national government coordination grants — would unlock the scale premium currently inaccessible within municipal boundaries.

**Third, redesign monitoring to serve fleet planning rather than facility auditing.** The finding that within-facility efficiency is essentially fixed means that annual facility-level efficiency monitoring provides little information for operational performance management. The same monitoring data, reframed as a fleet vintage register and retirement calendar, would serve the consolidation planning agenda far more effectively. The Ministry of the Environment should be resourced to develop and maintain such a register.

**Fourth, concentrate WtE investment incentives on large-scale modern facilities.** Feed-in tariffs and carbon credits for waste-derived electricity should be structured to reward efficiency as well as output, creating a premium for facilities that achieve above-average energy recovery per tonne. Current flat-rate incentive structures provide equal support per kWh regardless of efficiency, missing the opportunity to accelerate the shift of the fleet's economic center of gravity toward the high-performing segment.

## 6.4 Directions for Future Research

This thesis opens several lines of future inquiry. The most important is the governance of regional consolidation: what institutional arrangements — financial, contractual, political — have successfully enabled inter-municipal coordination in waste management, and which have failed? Case study analysis of successful regional consolidations in Japan would complement the statistical findings here with process understanding.

A second direction concerns the district heating question. Japan's major urban areas — particularly Sapporo and other Hokkaido cities, where winter heating demand is large — may offer underexplored opportunities for combined heat and power from waste incineration, analogous to the Nordic model. District heating integration could substantially increase total energy recovery even from existing facilities, potentially altering the efficiency landscape in ways the current analysis, which focuses only on electricity generation, cannot capture.

Third, the fixed-design finding raises a question about when and whether facility retrofits — major upgrades to boiler systems, turbine replacement, or combustion control modernization — are capable of partially overcoming the design constraint. The current dataset does not contain information on retrofit investments; a supplementary survey of facility modification histories would allow this question to be addressed directly.

Finally, as Japan's renewable energy penetration increases and the grid emission factor continues to decline, the avoided emissions value of waste-to-energy electricity generation will fall. Future work should model the trajectory of WtE's climate benefit under different decarbonization scenarios, to assess at what point the marginal contribution of WtE electricity to avoided emissions falls below the threshold that would justify continued investment in the sector.

## 6.5 Closing Statement

Japan built an incineration fleet for an era when burning waste was the best available option; the question its net-zero commitment now poses is not whether to change that fleet, but how quickly the country can replace what it has already locked in.
