%% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
Japan's incineration transition is a two-part problem: which facilities make an observed transition into power generation, and conditional on generation, which facilities achieve high energy recovery efficiency. Using a 20-year Ministry of the Environment panel (23,599 facility-year observations across 2,948 incinerators, FY2005--FY2024), this thesis separates those margins instead of treating waste-to-energy as a single category.

On the extensive margin, the coded full-fleet panel records 141 observed first-adoption events among 2,035 facilities first observed without power generation. In the lagged logit adoption hazard, facilities older than 10 years in the prior observed year are about 1.1--1.8 percentage points less likely than 0--10 year facilities to record transition into generation, while each additional 100 t/day of prior-year design capacity raises annual transition probability by about 0.5 percentage points. A conservative pathway audit suggests that reset- or rebuild-like events are the largest observed category, while stopping short of distinguishing replacement, major refurbishment, and new build.

On the intensive margin, conditional on generation, facility age is consistently negative, design capacity is positive, and capacity utilization is positive across the main and robustness specifications; mean efficiency declines from 0.400 MWh/t at facilities aged 0--10 years to 0.183 MWh/t at facilities over 30 years old. The within-to-total variance ratio of log-efficiency in the canonical generator frame is 0.1499, falling to 0.0956 in the post-Fukushima window, indicating that efficiency differences are dominated by cross-facility heterogeneity rather than large within-facility reversals over time.

At the fleet level, facility count fell from 1,318 to 1,014 between 2005 and 2024, the power-generating share rose from 21.6\% to 41.1\%, and gross avoided emissions from waste-to-energy reached about 4.6 million t-CO$_2$ in FY2024, though 59\% of active facilities still generated no electricity. The thesis therefore argues that Japan's net-zero challenge is twofold: observed transition into generation is selective on the extensive margin, while performance inside the generating segment changes within a bounded design envelope. The policy implication is correspondingly hierarchical: capital-side modernization appears most important for the weakest segment, while routing, utilization, and selective major refurbishment remain secondary but real levers.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} waste-to-energy, energy recovery efficiency, carbon lock-in, industrial ecology, Japan, fleet heterogeneity, infrastructure transition

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Research Problem}

Japan burns roughly 90,000 tonnes of municipal solid waste per day through the world's most incineration-dependent municipal waste system \citep{MOEJ2022}. Because incineration is embedded in local government infrastructure rather than treated as a temporary bridge technology, Japan's 2050 carbon-neutrality target creates a practical question that is narrower and more urgent than a generic debate about whether incineration should exist at all: which facilities are actually moving toward useful energy recovery, and which remain locked into low-value combustion?

That question has two margins that are often conflated. The first is extensive: which facilities first record a transition into power generation? The second is intensive: among facilities that already generate power, which characteristics predict energy recovery efficiency? Treating waste-to-energy as a single category obscures this distinction and blurs two different policy problems, fleet modernization and generator performance.

This thesis separates those margins using a 20-year facility-level panel from the Ministry of the Environment. The defended answer is straightforward. On the extensive margin, observed transition into generation is selective rather than diffuse: younger and larger facilities are more likely to record transition, while old small plants rarely do. On the intensive margin, efficiency among generators is strongly structured by age, scale, and utilization, and within-facility movement is small relative to between-facility differences. The argument that follows is therefore not that one mechanism such as replacement has been uniquely identified, but that Japan's net-zero challenge is two-part: selective modernization on the way into generation, and bounded responsiveness once generation already exists.

\subsection{Background: Japan's Waste-to-Energy System}

Japan's dependence on incineration emerged from rapid urbanization, limited landfill availability, and a regulatory emphasis on hygienic thermal treatment \citep{TabataTsai2016}. By the early 2000s, the country operated more than 1,300 general waste incineration facilities, overwhelmingly municipally owned or municipally contracted, and the system had become institutionally sticky as well as physically durable \citep{MOEJ2022}.

Technically, the relevant distinction is simple. A stoker-furnace incinerator becomes a waste-to-energy facility only when combustion heat is routed through boilers and turbines to produce electricity. Many Japanese incinerators, especially older ones, never made that conversion. The fleet has therefore always contained both generating and non-generating plants, a distinction that matters analytically rather than merely descriptively.

The balance has shifted, but unevenly. In 2005, about 21.6\% of operating incinerators generated electricity; by 2024, that share had risen to 41.1\%. Over the same period, facility count fell from 1,318 to 1,014. Japan's fleet is therefore consolidating and modernizing, but 59\% of active facilities still generate no electricity. The 2011 Fukushima shock and the later 2050 carbon-neutrality agenda increased the value of energy recovery, but neither shock by itself guaranteed that old small facilities would convert or that existing generators would close large vintage gaps through operations alone.

\subsection{The Problem: Heterogeneity Without Accountability}

Despite this transition pressure, the fleet has rarely been analyzed as a heterogeneous panel. Most existing work treats waste-to-energy as a category, compares national systems, or models aggregate emissions. What is missing is a facility-level account of who adopts generation at all, who remains outside it, and whether performance within the generating segment changes materially over time.

That gap matters because averages hide the distribution. A fleet average efficiency of 0.30 MWh/t could reflect a uniformly mediocre system or a sharply divided fleet of very strong and very weak performers. Those cases imply different policy responses. The distinction between adoption into generation and efficiency conditional on adoption matters for the same reason: if movement occurs mainly through younger, larger facilities entering the generating regime while within-generator performance changes modestly, then the main policy levers lie in capital decisions rather than in operating incentives alone.

The industrial ecology concept that organizes this problem is infrastructural lock-in \citep{Unruh2000, Seto2016}. In this setting, the relevant prediction is narrower than a broad philosophical claim about path dependence: if design vintage and scale dominate, then cross-facility heterogeneity should exceed within-facility change over time. This thesis uses the two-part design to test that prediction at the facility level while keeping the extensive-margin modernization problem analytically separate from the intensive-margin efficiency problem.

\subsection{Research Question and Objectives}

The primary research question guiding this thesis is:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{What predicts observed transition into power generation among coded facilities first seen without it, and conditional on power generation, what predicts energy recovery efficiency among power-generating incinerators?}
\end{quote}

To answer this, the thesis pursues four specific objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Describe fleet evolution}: document how the composition, scale, and power-generation penetration of Japan's incineration fleet has changed between FY2005 and FY2024.
  \item \textbf{Identify modernization at the extensive margin}: estimate which facilities first adopt power generation within the observation window, using the coded full-fleet panel.
  \item \textbf{Identify efficiency determinants at the intensive margin}: estimate the effects of facility age, design capacity, capacity utilization, waste heating value, and regional grid emission factors on energy recovery efficiency among operating power-generating facilities, using panel regression with cluster-robust inference.
  \item \textbf{Assess bounded responsiveness and policy implications}: determine whether efficiency changes meaningfully within generators over time, or remains constrained by design vintage, and translate the two-part evidence into recommendations consistent with Japan's net-zero trajectory.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Significance of the Study}

This study makes three contributions. First, it provides the first systematic multi-year facility-level analysis of Japan's incineration fleet that separates the extensive margin of power-generation adoption from the intensive margin of generator efficiency. That distinction matters empirically: the coded full-fleet panel speaks to who transitions into generation at all, while the generator-only frame speaks to conditional performance once generation exists.

Second, it applies infrastructure lock-in theory to a waste-sector setting with direct facility-level evidence. The adoption results show that observed transition into generation is concentrated among younger and larger facilities in the coded risk set, while the generator-only results show that within-facility efficiency changes relatively little over time relative to between-facility gaps. Together these findings are strongly consistent with a sector in which capital-side modernization matters more than large late-life reversals within existing plants, while still stopping short of claiming that one mechanism such as replacement is uniquely identified.

Third, the timing is policy-relevant. Japan is already consolidating its fleet under the broader 2050 carbon-neutrality agenda \citep{MOEJ2024}. This thesis provides evidence for where that agenda is already moving and where it remains constrained: adoption into generation is selective on the extensive margin, while routing and utilization improvements operate mainly within the already-generating segment.

\subsection{Thesis Structure}

The remainder of the thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 narrows the literature to the institutional context, lock-in framing, and efficiency determinants needed for the empirical design. Chapter 3 describes the two-part panel architecture, sample construction, and estimation strategy. Chapter 4 presents the descriptive fleet trends, adoption hazard, generator-efficiency results, avoided-emissions calculation, and robustness checks. Chapter 5 interprets those results for theory and policy, and Chapter 6 concludes.

Throughout, facilities with no power-generation capability are not treated as nuisance observations outside the design. They define the extensive-margin adoption problem, while the generator-efficiency regressions are then explicitly conditional on positive power generation and positive operating output. That distinction is central to the argument.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Overview}

This chapter narrows the literature to the pieces required for the empirical design: the institutional and technical development of Japan's waste-to-energy sector, the industrial ecology concepts of material metabolism and infrastructure lock-in, and the empirical literature on facility-level efficiency determinants.

\subsection{Japan's Waste-to-Energy System: Institutional Development and Technical Evolution}

\subsubsection{The Historical Foundation}

Japan's reliance on waste incineration reflects a durable combination of high population density, limited landfill availability, and a regulatory emphasis on hygienic waste containment \citep{Tanikawa2015}. The Waste Disposal and Public Cleansing Law of 1970 accelerated the shift toward controlled thermal treatment, and by the 1980s Japan had assembled the world's largest municipal incineration fleet \citep{TabataTsai2016}. The financing structure of that expansion matters for this thesis: facilities are overwhelmingly municipally owned or municipally contracted, construction has long been subsidized by the national government, and operating costs sit with local governments. That combination makes facilities politically and fiscally sticky once built.

\subsubsection{Technical Architecture: From Thermal Treatment to Energy Recovery}

Japan's fleet is dominated by the stoker furnace, a moving-grate system favored for handling heterogeneous municipal waste with high reliability \citep{Niessen2010}. Electricity generation requires routing combustion heat through a boiler and turbine-generator system, and the efficiency of that conversion depends on boiler pressure, turbine configuration, and internal parasitic loads. These are structural design features rather than minor add-ons. Larger facilities can justify more advanced steam systems, while older facilities built to earlier engineering standards are difficult to upgrade to modern performance levels because the boiler and turbine are integral to the plant's architecture \citep{Niessen2010, Sun2018}.

\subsubsection{Policy Context: Energy and Climate Drivers}

Two policy shifts frame the study window. First, Japan's Renewable Portfolio Standard and later Feed-in Tariff turned waste-derived electricity into a recognizable revenue stream for municipalities \citep{MOEJ2022}. Second, the Paris Agreement era and Japan's 2050 carbon-neutrality agenda increased pressure to justify incineration in climate terms \citep{MOEJ2024}. Together these shifts raised the value of energy recovery without removing the underlying institutional stickiness of the fleet.

\subsubsection{Fleet Modernization and Consolidation}

Published work on Japan's fleet trajectory is largely descriptive: facility count has fallen, the power-generating share has risen, and the system appears to be consolidating toward fewer larger plants \citep{MOEJ2022}. Internationally, Northern European WtE fleets achieve higher overall energy recovery because they combine larger facilities with district heating integration \citep{Munster2010}. Japan's limited district heating footprint means the relevant modernization margin is usually electrical efficiency rather than full combined heat-and-power performance.

\subsection{Industrial Ecology: Material Metabolism and Infrastructure Lock-In}

\subsubsection{Material Metabolism as Analytical Framework}

Industrial ecology treats waste management as part of the material metabolism of society: facilities receive waste, transform it, and emit energy, residues, and pollutants \citep{Ayres1994}. In that framing, energy recovery efficiency is a measure of how productively an incinerator converts residual waste into useful output. The perspective matters because it shifts attention from single facilities to the routing and coordination problem across the fleet.

\subsubsection{Infrastructure Lock-In: Mechanisms and Applications}

\emph{Lock-in}, in the industrial ecology literature, refers to the persistence of suboptimal technological trajectories due to the co-evolutionary entrenchment of physical infrastructure, institutions, and social practices \citep{Unruh2000}. The concept has roots in evolutionary economics, particularly \citet{Arthur1989}'s analysis of path dependence in technology adoption, but has been developed in the sustainability transitions literature to explain why sociotechnical systems resist change even when superior alternatives are available \citep{Geels2004}.

\emph{Carbon lock-in} is the specific case where energy and material systems dependent on fossil carbon persist beyond their economic or environmental optimum. \citet{Unruh2000} identifies three interacting mechanisms: technological lock-in (existing infrastructure embodies past design choices and is costly to abandon), institutional lock-in (regulatory frameworks, subsidy structures, and market rules co-evolve with dominant technologies), and cognitive lock-in (professional expertise, organizational routines, and public expectations are adapted to existing technology). All three mechanisms are visible in Japan's incineration sector: physical facilities with 30-year lifespans embody 1980s and 1990s engineering; municipal waste management governance is organized around existing facility locations; and the expertise of the waste management workforce is calibrated to stoker furnace operation.

\citet{Seto2016} argue that empirical progress on lock-in has been limited by the lack of facility-level tests. That is the main theoretical role of this thesis. If efficiency is primarily determined by design decisions made at construction, then within-facility variation over time should be small relative to between-facility variation across vintages and scales. The generator panel tests exactly that prediction.

\subsubsection{Circular Economy and Circular Transition}

Circular economy thinking prioritizes material recovery over energy recovery, and energy recovery over simple combustion without useful output \citep{EllenMacArthur2013}. In this thesis, the relevant question is narrower: given that Japan will continue to incinerate substantial residual waste, how heterogeneous is the transition from non-generating combustion to more efficient waste-to-energy?

\subsubsection{Stranded Assets and Infrastructure Transition}

The stranded-asset literature adds the cost side of transition \citep{Caldecott2016}. Old low-efficiency generators and ageing non-generators face decarbonization pressure before the end of their physical life, but not all are economically worth retrofitting. That framing helps explain why the transition problem is heterogeneous across municipalities rather than uniform across the fleet.

\subsection{Determinants of Energy Recovery Efficiency: The Empirical Literature}

\subsubsection{Scale Effects}

The relationship between facility scale and energy recovery efficiency is among the most consistently documented findings in the WtE literature. Larger facilities can support higher-pressure boiler systems, more sophisticated turbine configurations, better instrumentation and control, and more specialized technical staff, all of which contribute to higher electricity output per tonne of waste \citep{Genon2008}. Scale economies in boiler design are particularly significant: at small scale, heat losses relative to output are large, and the pressure ratings achievable are limited by the cost of pressure vessel fabrication. At large scale, fixed costs are amortized over more output and engineering can be optimized.

This relationship has been documented empirically in European contexts, where WtE facilities range from small municipal installations to very large urban facilities exceeding 1,000 tonnes per day of capacity. Studies of the Danish and German fleets find positive and statistically significant relationships between facility capacity and electrical efficiency \citep{Munster2010}. The current study extends this finding to Japan's fleet, where the scale range is similarly large, from facilities below 50 t/day to installations exceeding 500 t/day, and where the policy relevance of the scale relationship is directly tied to the consolidation agenda.

\subsubsection{Facility Age and Vintage Effects}

Age effects in WtE efficiency have been analyzed less systematically than scale effects, partly because most empirical studies use cross-sectional data that conflate age with design vintage. A facility built in 1985 operates with 1985 engineering standards regardless of how many years have passed; its low efficiency reflects design constraints, not degradation. This distinction between age-as-proxy-for-vintage and age-as-wear-and-tear is important but often blurred in the literature.

Some studies have examined facility degradation, the decline in performance of boiler systems and turbines as they age, finding modest efficiency losses that are partially offset by maintenance and component replacement \citep{Astrup2015}. However, the more robust finding across multiple contexts is that the vintage effect (newer facilities are built to more efficient designs) dominates the degradation effect. This is consistent with the lock-in hypothesis: design determines efficiency, and design improves with each generation of new construction.

The Japanese context adds a specific dimension: the rapid expansion of the fleet in the 1980s and 1990s means that a large cohort of facilities share a similar vintage and will reach end of life within a relatively compressed time window. This creates both a challenge (managing a wave of simultaneous retirements) and an opportunity (replacing them with modern, efficient facilities on a planned schedule).

\subsubsection{Capacity Utilization}

Capacity utilization, the ratio of actual throughput to design capacity, affects efficiency through thermodynamic and operational mechanisms. At low utilization, combustion systems operate outside their design range, making it difficult to maintain stable temperatures and steam parameters. Below certain throughput thresholds, facilities may switch from power-generating mode to simple heat disposal mode to maintain combustion stability. High utilization, conversely, allows facilities to operate at or near design conditions, maximizing steam generation and turbine output.

The empirical literature on utilization effects in WtE is sparse but generally consistent with a positive relationship \citep{Beylot2015}. The policy implication, that routing more waste to high-utilization facilities improves system-level efficiency, points toward regional consolidation as a policy lever. In Japan's highly fragmented waste management system, where municipal jurisdiction boundaries often limit inter-municipality waste transfers, this implication is institutionally challenging to implement even where the technical case is clear.

\subsubsection{Waste Composition and Heating Value}

The energy content of waste, typically measured as lower heating value (LHV) in MJ/kg, is a natural candidate for an efficiency determinant. Higher-energy waste should, all else equal, generate more steam per unit mass, and hence more electricity. However, the empirical literature on this relationship is surprisingly ambiguous \citep{Astrup2009}.

Several mechanisms complicate the straightforward prediction. First, modern furnace control systems are designed to maintain stable combustion temperatures across a range of waste heating values, effectively buffering the direct relationship between waste energy content and boiler output. Second, high-heating-value waste may require additional cooling (through waste mixing or steam injection) to prevent equipment damage, reducing net efficiency gains. Third, the heating value data available in administrative datasets, typically estimated from waste composition surveys rather than direct measurement, may not accurately reflect actual combustion conditions.

This thesis finds that heating value is not a statistically significant predictor of efficiency in any of the main or robustness specifications, a result that aligns with the more skeptical strand of the literature and has important implications for data collection policy.

\subsubsection{Regional and Grid Factors}

Some studies include regional variables to capture differences in grid emission factors, energy prices, or climate conditions that might affect operational decisions. The theoretical case for a grid emission factor effect on \emph{efficiency} (as opposed to \emph{avoided emissions}) is not straightforward, a facility's efficiency in converting waste to electricity should not depend on what that electricity displaces in the grid. However, there may be indirect effects through incentive structures: in regions with high grid emission factors, the value of waste-derived electricity is higher, potentially incentivizing investment in efficiency upgrades or attracting more capital to new facility construction.

This thesis includes regional grid emission factors as a control variable and finds that the coefficient sign is unstable across model specifications, positive in some and negative in others, suggesting that this variable does not capture a stable efficiency determinant. This instability is itself informative: it suggests that regional energy market conditions are not a reliable lever for improving incineration efficiency, and that the primary determinants are physical (age, scale) rather than economic.

\subsection{The Research Gap: Fleet-Level Heterogeneity Analysis}

The literature surveyed above shares a common limitation: it treats WtE efficiency determinants as properties of technology types or average systems, rather than as characteristics that vary systematically across a heterogeneous fleet over time. Cross-sectional studies capture between-facility variation at a point in time but cannot assess whether the relationships are stable, how they change as the fleet modernizes, or what within-facility dynamics look like over time. Technology-level analyses abstract from the facility-level variation that is, empirically, the main source of efficiency differences in the Japanese fleet.

Three gaps are particularly important. First, no published study has applied panel regression methods to Japan's full incineration fleet using facility-level administrative data over a multi-decade period. The available data source, the Ministry of the Environment's General Waste Treatment Survey, covers all operating facilities annually and contains the variables needed for this analysis, but it has not been exploited for this purpose in the academic literature. Second, the question of within-facility efficiency dynamics, whether facilities improve their performance over their operational lives, has not been addressed empirically for Japan. This question is theoretically central to the lock-in debate: if within-facility improvement is negligible, then operational optimization policies are ineffective and retirement/replacement is the only viable path. Third, the fleet-level implications of heterogeneity, the distribution of avoided emissions across facility types, the concentration of efficiency gains in a small number of large modern plants, have not been quantified.

This thesis addresses all three gaps. It contributes a panel dataset of 23,599 facility-year observations, regression estimates robust to multiple model specifications, and a framework for translating fleet heterogeneity findings into net-zero policy recommendations grounded in industrial ecology theory.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Overview}

This chapter describes the data, variable construction, sample selection, and estimation strategy used to answer the research question. The empirical design has two linked parts. The first explains modernization on the extensive margin, which facilities first adopt power generation within the observation window. The second explains cross-facility variation in energy recovery efficiency conditional on adoption. The methodological challenge is to keep those two margins distinct while still drawing coherent conclusions about the fleet.

The chapter proceeds as follows. Section 3.2 describes the data source and its structure. Section 3.3 explains the cleaning and panel construction steps. Section 3.4 defines the dependent and independent variables. Section 3.5 describes the two-part estimation strategy and the rationale for model selection. Section 3.6 details the robustness checks for the generator-efficiency models.

\subsection{Data Source}

\subsubsection{The General Waste Treatment Survey}

The primary data source is the Ministry of the Environment (MOE) General Waste Treatment Survey (\emph{Ippan Haikibutsu Shori Jittai Chosa}), an annual administrative survey covering all operating general waste treatment facilities in Japan. The survey collects facility-level data on waste input volumes, treatment method, facility capacity, operational status, and, for energy-recovering facilities, electricity generation and fuel consumption. The survey has been conducted annually since the 1960s in various forms; the analysis in this thesis uses the fiscal year (FY) 2005 through FY2024 editions, covering 20 years of fleet evolution \citep{MOEJ2022}. The FY2005 start date is chosen deliberately: Japan's Air Pollution Control Law and Waste Management Law dioxin-emission standards, enacted in 1999 and phased in through the early 2000s, triggered a wave of retrofits, closures, and replacements that only stabilised around the middle of the decade. Starting in FY2005 ensures that the fleet under analysis operates under a stable regulatory regime for dioxin and fine-particulate emissions throughout the observation window, so that the estimated age and capacity effects are not confounded with a compliance-driven restructuring episode.

The survey is conducted by prefectural governments, which collect data from individual municipalities, which in turn collect from individual facilities. This administrative chain means the data are subject to reporting conventions that vary somewhat across prefectures and years. The cleaning process described in Section 3.3 addresses the most significant of these inconsistencies.

The appeal of the General Waste Treatment Survey for this research is threefold. First, it is near-census in coverage: because facility operation requires permits under the Waste Disposal Law and because municipal expenditure on waste management is reported to the national government, there is strong incentive for accurate and complete reporting. Second, it contains the specific variables needed for this analysis, waste volume, facility capacity, electricity generation, and facility age, at the facility level over multiple years. Third, it is publicly available, making the analysis replicable by other researchers and policy analysts.

\subsubsection{Panel Structure}

The raw dataset, after merging annual survey files for FY2005--FY2024, contains observations at the facility-year level. Not all facilities appear in all years, both because of entries (new facilities beginning operation) and exits (facility closures). This creates an unbalanced panel, a structure in which different facilities contribute different numbers of years of data.

The full panel contains 23,599 facility-year observations across 2,948 unique facilities. The average facility contributes 8.0 years of data, though this varies substantially: some facilities appear in all 20 years, while others appear for only one or two years, typically corresponding to opening or closure years in which full-year data are not available. After restricting to observations with an official facility code present, the coded full-fleet frame used for the extensive-margin analysis contains 19,827 observations across the same 2,948 facilities.

\subsection{Data Cleaning and Panel Construction}

\subsubsection{Facility Identification and Linking}

The most significant data quality challenge in working with the General Waste Treatment Survey is facility identification across years. Official facility codes are present for most, but not all, observations. The canonical analysis therefore takes a conservative approach: both the extensive-margin adoption model and the generator-efficiency panel use only observations with an official facility code present when constructing panel identifiers and clustering standard errors, while retaining the larger uncoded set for high-level descriptive counts. The resulting attrition is documented explicitly in the analysis sample report generated by the pipeline rather than being absorbed silently through ad hoc filtering.

Municipal mergers (\emph{heisei no daigappei}, the wave of municipal consolidations that reduced Japan's municipality count from approximately 3,200 to 1,700, peaking in 2004--2006 with tail-end mergers extending into the early 2010s \citep{Rausch2006}) create particular challenges because facilities may be reported under different administrative units before and after merger without any change in physical characteristics. These cases were identified and corrected manually where possible.

\subsubsection{Variable Cleaning and Outlier Treatment}

Several variables required cleaning before analysis. Facility age is floored at zero for a small number of one-year commissioning mismatches. For the extensive-margin model, the coded full-fleet panel is converted into an observed first-adoption risk set: facilities already generating power in their first observed year are treated as left-censored and excluded from the adoption hazard, while facilities first observed without power generation remain in the risk set until they either adopt generation or leave the panel. This yields 13,770 facility-year observations across 2,035 facilities, with 141 observed first-adoption events and 913 left-censored facilities already generating in their first observed year. The adoption model itself is estimated on a smaller lagged frame of 11,717 facility-year observations across 1,915 facilities and 140 events, because the first observed at-risk year for each facility has no lagged predictors and 18 additional rows lack prior-year age or capacity values.

Waste input volume and electricity generation are reported in absolute units (tonnes and kWh, respectively) that must be combined to compute efficiency. Observations with non-positive waste input or non-positive electricity output are excluded from the operating power-generation sample used for descriptive and regression analysis. Capacity utilization is capped at 1.0 at analysis time to prevent emergency-overload years from dominating the coefficient scale.

The efficiency variable (electricity generation divided by waste input, in MWh per tonne) is winsorized by clipping to fixed engineering bounds of [0.01, 0.80] MWh/t. In the canonical generator regression frame, the dependent variable is therefore defined on power-generating observations with positive throughput, positive electricity output, an official facility code present, and all model covariates observed. This yields a final regression sample of 5,683 facility-year observations across 1,016 facilities.

The choice of 0.80 MWh/t as the upper winsorizing bound reflects the physical limits of WtE electricity generation: the theoretical maximum conversion efficiency of a stoker furnace system, at realistic waste heating values, is approximately 0.9--1.0 MWh/t, and values above 0.80 almost certainly reflect reporting inconsistencies rather than genuine performance. The lower bound of 0.01 MWh/t captures minimal but real power generation, excluding only observations that are effectively zero.

\subsubsection{Heating Value Estimation}

Waste heating value is not directly reported in the General Waste Treatment Survey. For some years and facilities, it can be estimated from supplementary survey data on waste composition; for others, it must be inferred from national or regional composition averages. Given the known limitations of this approach, the indirect measurement introduces substantial noise, heating value should be treated as an approximate control variable rather than a precisely measured independent variable. The finding that heating value is statistically insignificant in all model specifications may partly reflect this measurement noise, though the thermodynamic arguments for insignificance (Section 2.4.4) suggest genuine ambiguity.

\subsection{Variable Definitions}

\subsubsection{Dependent Variables}

The thesis uses two dependent variables aligned with the two empirical margins of interest.

\textbf{Extensive margin: first adoption of power generation.} The adoption outcome equals 1 in the fiscal year a facility is first observed with power-generation capability, conditional on having been observed without power generation in all prior years of the panel, and 0 otherwise. The extensive-margin model is therefore an observed first-adoption hazard estimated on the coded full-fleet risk set.

\textbf{Intensive margin: energy recovery efficiency conditional on generation.} The second dependent variable is the natural logarithm of energy recovery efficiency, defined as:

\begin{equation}
\text{log\_efficiency}_{it} = \ln\!\left(\frac{\text{Electricity generated}_{it} \text{ (MWh)}}{\text{Waste processed}_{it} \text{ (tonnes)}}\right)
\end{equation}

The log transformation serves two purposes. First, it addresses the right-skewed distribution of raw efficiency values, a small number of high-performing facilities pull the distribution rightward, and the log transformation produces a more symmetric distribution better suited to linear regression. Second, it yields coefficients interpretable as approximate proportional effects: a coefficient of $-0.028$ on facility age means that each additional year of age is associated with approximately a 2.8\% lower efficiency level. Robustness checks using the raw (untransformed) dependent variable are reported in Section 4.5.

\subsubsection{Independent Variables}

\textbf{Facility age} is measured as the number of years since the facility began operation as of the observation year. This is derived from the facility's reported start date and the fiscal year of each observation. Age is the primary variable of interest, motivated by the lock-in hypothesis that efficiency is determined by design vintage.

\textbf{Design capacity} is measured in tonnes per day (t/day) as reported in the survey. This reflects the facility's rated processing capacity, not its actual throughput. To facilitate interpretation, capacity is included in units of 100 t/day, so that the regression coefficient represents the efficiency gain associated with 100 additional t/day of design capacity.

    \textbf{Capacity utilization} is defined as the ratio of actual annual waste input to theoretical maximum annual input (design capacity $\times$ 365 days), expressed as a proportion. This variable captures how intensively a facility is operated relative to its design specification. It is bounded between 0 and 1 in theory, though values slightly above 1.0 are possible when facilities accept emergency waste transfers beyond design capacity; such observations are capped at 1.0 in the analysis frame.

\textbf{Waste heating value} is the estimated lower heating value of the waste stream processed, measured in MJ/kg. As noted above, this variable is estimated rather than directly measured and should be treated with appropriate caution.

\textbf{Grid emission factor} captures the carbon intensity of the regional electricity grid, measured in kg-CO$_2$/kWh. This variable varies across Japan's nine major regional electricity utility service areas and changes over time, particularly following the Fukushima-induced nuclear shutdowns and subsequent restructuring. It enters the model as a proxy for the value of waste-derived electricity generation in carbon terms and for regional energy market conditions.

\subsection{Estimation Strategy}

\subsubsection{Two-Part Estimation Framework}

The empirical design separates the extensive margin of modernization from the intensive margin of generator performance.

\textbf{Extensive margin: adoption hazard.} The first-adoption model is:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Pr(\text{AdoptPower}_{it}=1 \mid X_{i,t-1}) ={}& \operatorname{logit}^{-1}\bigl(\alpha + \delta_1\,\text{AgeBand}_{i,t-1} \\
& \quad + \delta_2\,\text{Capacity}_{i,t-1} + \lambda_p + \gamma_t\bigr)
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $\text{AdoptPower}_{it}$ equals 1 if facility $i$ is first observed adopting power generation in fiscal year $t$, $\lambda_p$ is a prefecture fixed effect, and $\gamma_t$ is a year fixed effect. The predictors are lagged one observed year so the adoption coefficients are read as pre-transition correlates rather than same-year redesign attributes. The main table reports a discrete-time logit hazard with standard errors clustered at facility level and average marginal effects translated into percentage points for interpretation; lagged complementary log-log and lagged linear probability variants are used as robustness checks and return the same sign pattern. Age is entered in bands rather than linearly because the event rates are sharply nonlinear across the facility life cycle.

\textbf{Intensive margin: generator-efficiency panel.} Conditional on generation, the baseline estimating equation is:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\ln(\text{efficiency}_{it}) ={} & \alpha + \beta_1\,\text{Age}_{it} + \beta_2\,\text{Capacity}_{it} + \beta_3\,\text{Utilization}_{it} \\
                                & + \beta_4\,\text{HeatingValue}_{it} + \beta_5\,\text{GridEF}_{it} + \gamma_t + \epsilon_{it}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

where $i$ indexes facilities, $t$ indexes fiscal years, $\gamma_t$ is a year fixed effect capturing common shocks (e.g., the Fukushima event, changes in the feed-in tariff), and $\epsilon_{it}$ is the idiosyncratic error term. Model 1 (pooled OLS) imposes $\gamma_t = 0$ for all $t$, so the year fixed effects drop out; Models 2 and 4 estimate $\gamma_t$ as a full set of year dummies; Model 3 (random effects without year FE) again imposes $\gamma_t = 0$. The key parameters of interest are $\beta_1$ through $\beta_3$, corresponding to age, scale, and utilization within the generator sample.

\subsubsection{Model Specifications}

One extensive-margin model and four intensive-margin specifications are estimated.

\textbf{Adoption Model (Extensive Margin):} A lagged first-adoption logit hazard on coded facilities first observed without power generation, with prior-year age-band indicators, prior-year design capacity, year fixed effects, prefecture fixed effects, and facility-clustered standard errors; the same lagged specification is also checked with complementary log-log and lagged linear probability variants.

\textbf{Model 1 (Pooled OLS):} No facility or year fixed effects. Identifies the relationships between facility characteristics and efficiency from variation across all facility-year observations. Standard errors are clustered by facility to account for serial correlation within facilities.

\textbf{Model 2 (Year Fixed Effects):} Includes year dummy variables to control for time-varying common factors. This absorbs the Fukushima shock, changes in feed-in tariff rates, and other year-specific influences on efficiency. Standard errors remain clustered by facility.

\textbf{Model 3 (Random Effects):} Assumes facility-specific unobservables are uncorrelated with the regressors (the Gauss-Markov assumption extended to panel data). Random effects estimation is more efficient than pooled OLS when the assumption holds, because it uses both within- and between-facility variation.

\textbf{Model 4 (Year Fixed Effects + Random Effects):} The most complete specification, combining time controls with random facility effects.

\subsubsection{Why Not Facility Fixed Effects?}

The natural candidate for the generator-efficiency panel is the facility fixed-effects (FE) estimator, which controls for all time-invariant facility characteristics by differencing out the facility mean. However, facility fixed effects are inappropriate in this context for a specific reason: the primary variable of interest, facility age, increases deterministically by one unit per year for every facility. In a fixed-effects model, the age coefficient is identified entirely from within-facility deviations of age from its facility-specific mean, which for a variable that increases by 1 per year is equivalent to estimating the coefficient from a linear trend. This is nearly collinear with year fixed effects and produces imprecise estimates.

More fundamentally, the ratio of within-to-total variance for log(efficiency) in the canonical regression frame is 0.1499, meaning that roughly 85\% of the variation in efficiency is \emph{between} facilities rather than \emph{within} facilities over time. The ratio remains low across subsamples: 0.1795 in the pre-Fukushima window (FY2005--FY2011) and 0.0956 in the post-Fukushima window (FY2012--FY2024). This is a substantively important descriptive finding in itself: within the canonical generator sample, facilities do not exhibit large year-to-year efficiency movements relative to the size of the cross-facility differences. Following \citet{Seto2016}, who define infrastructural lock-in as the condition under which facility-level performance remains stable in the face of changing external incentives, the persistence of low within/total ratios across the full sample and the post-Fukushima period is strongly consistent with the lock-in mechanism at facility granularity, not merely a statement about fixed-effects mechanics, though it is not by itself a causal estimate of irreversibility or of the effect size of operational intervention. It also means that facility fixed effects would lean on the smaller part of the signal while the age/year collinearity problem remains. The random effects estimator, which exploits between-facility variation while modeling within-facility observations as correlated draws from a facility-specific distribution, is therefore retained as part of the primary comparison set rather than replaced by two-way FE.

For transparency, a Hausman test applied to Models 3 and 4 does formally reject the random-effects null in favour of facility FE ($\chi^2 \approx 173$, $p < 0.0001$). This is noted for completeness but does not drive the estimator choice in this thesis, because the Hausman test evaluates whether RE is consistent under the idealised assumption that coefficients are actually identifiable within the FE framework, a precondition that fails here for exactly the two reasons elaborated below. The methodological pivot away from facility FE as the primary estimator therefore rests on two substantive justifications, not on the outcome of a Hausman test. First, the collinearity problem: facility age increases deterministically by one unit per year, making it nearly collinear with year fixed effects in a two-way FE specification, so the age coefficient is poorly identified within the FE framework. Second, the variance problem: the within-to-total variation ratio of 0.1499 means that facility FE leans on the smaller part of the variation in the dependent variable. These are separate problems, the first is an identification issue and the second is a power issue, but both point in the same direction: facility FE cannot cleanly recover the parameters of substantive interest in this dataset, because those parameters (age, scale, location) are primarily between-facility features that facility FE differences away. Pooled OLS with cluster-robust standard errors and random effects (which models facility heterogeneity as a random component rather than absorbing it through differencing) are therefore the appropriate primary estimators for answering the research question, with facility FE reported as a reference point rather than the core design. Standard-error and specification choices throughout follow \citet{Wooldridge2010}.

\subsubsection{Inference}

All standard errors are clustered at the facility level to account for the correlation of errors across years within the same facility. Clustering is the appropriate response to the likely autocorrelation of facility-level unobservables across years: a facility with unobserved characteristics that raise efficiency in year $t$ is likely to exhibit the same advantage in year $t+1$, and conventional standard errors that assume independence would understate the true sampling uncertainty.

Statistical significance is assessed at the 1\%, 5\%, and 10\% levels, but given the size of the canonical regression frame (5,683 observations), the practical emphasis is on the magnitude and consistency of estimated coefficients rather than on $p$-values alone. Coefficients that remain directionally stable across the four main specifications (Section 3.5.2) and the eight robustness specifications (Section 3.6) are treated as robust findings; coefficients with unstable signs or magnitudes are highlighted as unreliable even when individually significant.

\subsection{Robustness Design}

The robustness strategy addresses four potential concerns about the primary estimates.

\textbf{Pre/post-Fukushima split.} The 2011 Fukushima disaster represents a major exogenous shock to Japan's energy system. If the determinants of efficiency changed meaningfully between the pre-Fukushima (FY2005--FY2011) and post-Fukushima (FY2012--FY2024) periods, pooling the full period could be misleading. One plausible channel is that the increased value of electricity generation changed operational incentives. To check this, Models 1 and 2 are estimated separately on pre- and post-Fukushima subsamples, yielding four additional specifications (Robustness Specifications R1--R4).

\textbf{Capacity subsample analysis.} If the relationships between efficiency and its predictors are nonlinear, for example, if scale effects are concentrated in the transition from small to medium facilities but absent at large scale, then linear specifications may misrepresent the underlying relationships. To explore this, the pooled OLS specification is estimated separately for facilities in the bottom tercile and the top tercile of the capacity distribution (Robustness Specifications R5 and R6). Comparing these endpoint estimates is sufficient to detect meaningful nonlinearity in the scale effect; the middle tercile is omitted because its coefficients fall by construction between the two endpoints and add no additional information for this purpose.

\textbf{Raw dependent variable.} The log transformation of the dependent variable is standard but involves an assumption that the underlying relationship is multiplicative rather than additive. Robustness Specifications R7--R8 therefore replicate Models 1 and 2 with the winsorized raw efficiency as the dependent variable, allowing assessment of whether the log transformation materially affects the substantive conclusions.

The eight robustness specifications collectively constitute a systematic assessment of the sensitivity of the main findings. The age, capacity, and utilization coefficients are consistently signed and statistically significant across all eight robustness specifications (see Section 4.5); the heating value and grid emission factor coefficients are not.

\subsubsection{Within-Facility Efficiency Dynamics}

A crucial descriptive finding, one that motivates the choice of random effects over facility fixed effects, is the near-complete absence of within-facility efficiency change over time.

The ratio of within-facility variance to total variance for log(efficiency) in the canonical regression frame is 0.1499. This means that roughly 85\% of all variation in efficiency in the dataset is \emph{between} facilities (explained by differences in age, scale, design, and operating conditions at construction) and only about 15\% is \emph{within} facilities over time. The ratio remains low across subsamples: 0.1795 in the pre-Fukushima period (FY2005--FY2011) and 0.0956 in the post-Fukushima period (FY2012--FY2024). Both subsample ratios and the pooled ratio tell the same substantive story: within-facility efficiency varies much less than efficiency differs across facilities. In practical terms, if you know a facility's efficiency in one year, you can predict its efficiency in subsequent years with high accuracy; it will not meaningfully improve or decline.

This is a descriptive result, not yet an interpretive conclusion. Its role in Chapter 4 is to document how the generator sample behaves and to motivate why cross-facility structure remains central to the empirical design. The lock-in interpretation of this pattern is taken up in Chapter 5.

\subsubsection{Facility Age}

Facility age is the most consistent and substantively important predictor of energy recovery efficiency. Across the four main specifications, the coefficient on age ranges from $-0.019$ to $-0.035$ and is statistically significant at the 0.1\% level in all cases. The estimates differ across pooled OLS and random-effects formulations, but the sign is stable and the year-FE variants remain tightly clustered around $-0.033$ to $-0.035$, confirming that the age effect is not an artifact of one particular modeling choice.

The economic and engineering interpretation of a coefficient near $-0.03$ is that each additional year of facility age is associated with roughly 3\% lower efficiency, holding capacity, utilization, and other covariates constant. Over the range from a new facility (age = 0) to a 30-year-old facility (age = 30), this implies a log-scale change of about $-0.90$, equivalent to $\exp(-0.90) \approx 0.41$, i.e., a 30-year-old facility is predicted to operate at roughly 40\% of an otherwise comparable new facility's efficiency. This remains steeper than the raw cohort ratio in Table~\ref{tab:efficiency_age}, because the regression isolates the age effect from the confounding influence of scale.

\subsubsection{Design Capacity}

Design capacity exerts a strong positive effect on efficiency. The coefficient per 100 t/day of design capacity ranges from $+0.041$ to $+0.103$ across the four main specifications, all significant at the 0.1\% level. A facility with 300 t/day of design capacity is therefore predicted to have approximately 8--23\% higher efficiency than an otherwise comparable facility with 100 t/day, depending on specification.

This scale effect reflects the engineering economics of boiler and turbine design discussed in Section 2.4.1. At larger scale, fixed costs of higher-pressure boiler systems are amortized over more tonnes processed, and the minimum efficient scale of turbine-generator sets becomes achievable. The coefficient magnitude suggests that scale effects are strong throughout the observed capacity range, there is no indication of diminishing returns within the distribution of Japanese facilities.

\subsubsection{Capacity Utilization}

Capacity utilization is a consistently strong positive predictor of efficiency. Coefficients range from $+0.541$ to $+0.779$, significant at the 0.1\% level in all four specifications. Moving from 60\% utilization to 80\% utilization, a 20 percentage point increase, is associated with approximately 11--17\% higher efficiency. Moving from 50\% to 90\% utilization is associated with approximately 24--37\% higher efficiency.

This finding reflects thermodynamic realities: facilities operating near their design capacity maintain more stable combustion conditions and steam parameters, maximizing electricity output per tonne of waste. At low utilization rates, facilities may operate in partial-load regimes that reduce boiler efficiency, or may shut down boiler systems entirely during low-input periods, reducing effective electricity generation relative to waste processed.

\subsubsection{Heating Value}

Heating value is not statistically significant in any of the four main specifications. The point estimates are close to zero (ranging from $+0.001$ to $+0.003$), but with standard errors of comparable magnitude, none approaches conventional significance thresholds. This null result is robust across the main specifications and reinforces the treatment of heating value as a noisy control variable rather than a substantive driver of the argument.

The insignificance of heating value is theoretically plausible, for reasons discussed in Section 2.4.4: modern combustion control systems buffer the relationship between waste energy content and boiler output, and high-LHV waste may require cooling interventions that reduce net recovery. However, measurement noise, because heating value is estimated from composition surveys rather than direct measurement, cannot be ruled out as a contributing factor, so the null should be treated cautiously rather than over-interpreted.

\subsubsection{Grid Emission Factor}

The grid emission factor coefficient is unstable across specifications in a way that makes it uninterpretable as a causal effect. In Model 1 (pooled OLS without year fixed effects), the coefficient is positive ($+0.318$) but not significant at conventional levels; in Model 2 (with year fixed effects), it flips to negative ($-0.447$) and remains statistically insignificant. The RE model produces a large positive estimate, but that estimate again disappears once year effects are added. This instability suggests that the grid emission factor is not measuring a stable causal relationship but is instead proxying for broader year-varying structure in the fleet and electricity system.

This variable is therefore retained as a control but not interpreted causally.

\subsection{Avoided Emissions Calculation}

\subsubsection{Method}

The avoided CO$_2$ emissions attributable to waste-to-energy generation are calculated as:

\begin{equation}
\text{Avoided CO}_2 = \text{Electricity generated (MWh)} \times \text{Grid emission factor (t-CO}_2\text{/MWh)}
\end{equation}

This calculation credits WtE with displacing grid electricity that would otherwise have been generated from the average fuel mix in the regional grid, at the prevailing emission factor. It does not account for the process emissions from waste combustion itself, which involve both biogenic CO$_2$ (from organic waste) and fossil CO$_2$ (from plastic, synthetic textiles, and other petroleum-derived materials). The gross avoided emissions calculation presented here is therefore an upper bound on the net climate benefit of waste-to-energy electricity generation.

\subsubsection{FY2024 Fleet-Level Estimates}

In FY2024, Japan's 416 power-generating incineration facilities collectively generated approximately 9.3 TWh of electricity from approximately 31 million tonnes of waste processed (computed from this thesis's dataset). Applying the average regional grid emission factor of 0.49 t-CO$_2$/MWh yields:

\begin{equation}
\text{Avoided CO}_2 \approx 9.3 \text{ TWh} \times 0.49 \text{ t-CO}_2\text{/MWh} \approx 4.56 \text{ million t-CO}_2
\end{equation}

Rounding to one significant figure: approximately \textbf{4.6 million t-CO$_2$} in gross avoided emissions in FY2024. This figure represents an upper bound on the net climate benefit, as it does not deduct process CO$_2$ from waste combustion (both biogenic and fossil fractions). The net benefit depends on the fossil carbon content of the waste stream, which is not available at the facility level in this dataset.

\subsubsection{Distribution of Avoided Emissions}

This aggregate figure conceals extreme concentration. The top 10\% of FY2024 power-generating facilities by electricity generation (about 42 plants, out of the 416 active in FY2024) account for an estimated 55--60\% of total avoided emissions, reflecting the combination of large capacity, high utilization, and recent vintage that characterizes the high-efficiency segment of the fleet. The bottom 50\% of power-generating facilities, predominantly small, older installations, collectively contribute approximately 15\% of avoided emissions despite representing half the facility count.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Overview}

This chapter interprets the two-part empirical design in substantive terms: modernization on the extensive margin, bounded responsiveness within the generator sample, and the policy consequences of scale, utilization, and fleet age structure.

\subsection{Modernization Occurs on the Extensive Margin}

The first interpretive claim of this thesis is that fleet modernization must be analyzed separately from generator efficiency. The coded full-fleet panel does not show a broad pattern of late conversion among old small plants. Instead, observed transition into generation is concentrated among facilities that were already younger and larger before the transition year. That matters because it gives the fleet-level argument an evidentiary base that is separate from the generator-only efficiency model: the modernization story is already selective before the conditional efficiency question is even asked.

The pathway audit sharpens that point without overstating mechanism. Reset- or rebuild-like events form the largest observed category, continuity-type upgrades remain material, and a residual group is left unresolved rather than forced into a stronger story. The appropriate conclusion is therefore narrower than ``replacement proven'' but stronger than a generic modernization narrative: the coded panel supports capital-side selectivity on the way into generation, not diffuse late-life conversion across the old small-plant segment.

\subsection{Bounded Responsiveness Within the Generator Sample}

Within the canonical generator frame, the most consequential fact is not a single regression coefficient but the low within-facility variation ratio of 0.1499 for log-efficiency. Roughly 85\% of the variation in energy recovery efficiency is between facilities rather than within facilities over time, and the ratio remains low in both the pre-Fukushima and post-Fukushima subsamples. This does not prove infrastructural lock-in by itself, but it is difficult to reconcile with a story in which large late-life efficiency reversals are common within the generating segment.

That descriptive pattern is strongly consistent with the lock-in mechanism described by \citet{Unruh2000} and \citet{Seto2016}: facility performance appears to respond within an envelope set largely by design vintage, scale, and configuration at construction. Two alternative interpretations remain possible and should be kept explicit. First, correlated measurement error in reported electricity and waste throughput could mechanically compress within-facility variation. Second, within-facility improvements may be technically possible but simply not widely pursued under the economics of the period. The thesis cannot fully adjudicate between those alternatives with the available data, so the strongest defended claim is calibrated: the evidence supports bounded responsiveness within the generator sample rather than causal proof of irreversibility. The convergence with recent Chinese facility-level evidence \citep{Cui2026} strengthens that interpretation, but does not remove the identification limit.

The practical implication is narrower than a blanket replacement claim but still substantive. The adoption model shows that old and small facilities rarely make an observed transition into generation in the coded panel. Within the already-generating segment, the efficiency model shows that operating adjustments alone are unlikely to close large vintage gaps. The evidence therefore supports a policy hierarchy in which capital-side modernization is the strongest implication for the weakest segment, while routing, utilization, and selective major refurbishment remain secondary levers rather than being dismissed entirely.

This conclusion aligns with the ``stranded asset'' framing from the sustainability transitions literature \citep{Caldecott2016}. Facilities in the 25--35 year range appear constrained less by one failing component than by an inherited design package of boiler, turbine, and waste-handling choices \citep{Niessen2010}. Targeted retrofits remain technically possible, but the 20-year panel does not show them reshaping the efficiency distribution at fleet scale. For the weakest segment, the largest gains therefore still pass mainly through major capital renewal, while operational adjustment remains a secondary lever.

\subsubsection{What ``Design-Determined'' Means in Practice}

If efficiency is strongly design-conditioned, annual monitoring has more value for fleet planning than for expecting large year-to-year reversals in mature plants. The same reporting system that supports accountability and billing can also function as a vintage register and retirement-planning tool, identifying persistently weak cohorts rather than treating every year as a new opportunity for large standalone reversals.

\subsection{Scale, Consolidation, and the Governance Challenge}

The positive effect of design capacity on efficiency gives empirical support to the consolidation trend already underway in Japan's fleet. Replacing small old facilities with larger modern ones combines a scale premium with a vintage premium, and the rise in mean efficiency among power-generating facilities over the sample is consistent with that transition.

That scale premium is not trivial. In the main specifications, each additional 100 t/day of design capacity is associated with roughly 4--10\% higher efficiency on the log scale, so the difference between a 100 t/day plant and a 300 t/day plant is economically meaningful rather than marginal.

The constraint is institutional rather than thermodynamic. Waste planning remains fragmented across municipalities whose boundaries often do not match efficient waste sheds, so routing waste toward larger facilities requires shared governance, cost allocation, and siting agreements that are difficult to sustain \citep{Sakai2008, TabataTsai2016, Sun2018}. National grants have supported cooperation \citep{MOEJ2024}, but the main lesson is straightforward: the scale premium exists, yet capturing it depends on regional coordination rather than facility engineering alone.

\subsubsection{Consolidation in Practice: Heat Integration as a Stress Test}

The scale premium only becomes a policy pathway if institutions can support consolidation in practice. The few Japanese cases where waste-to-energy has been integrated with district heating, mainly in northern cities such as Sapporo, are useful only as a stress test showing how rare multi-municipal energy integration remains \citep{TabataTsai2016}. The implication for this thesis is narrow: the regression identifies a real scale premium, but current institutions still make region-wide coordination difficult.

\subsubsection{The Utilization Dimension}

The strong utilization effect opens a second, complementary lever: routing more waste to existing high-efficiency plants rather than relying only on new construction. That path is attractive because it is faster and cheaper than replacement, but it runs into the same governance obstacle as consolidation itself: municipalities are often reluctant to send waste to neighboring facilities if doing so weakens the fiscal case for their own plant. In practice, the utilization result reinforces the case for regional coordination rather than replacing it.

\subsection{The Fukushima Effect: Energy Markets and Fleet Responsiveness}

The pre- versus post-Fukushima robustness checks show bounded rather than zero responsiveness. The age coefficient moves from about $-0.042$ to $-0.043$ in the pre-Fukushima models to about $-0.032$ to $-0.033$ post-Fukushima, while the utilization coefficient rises from about $+0.63$--$+0.74$ to about $+0.82$--$+0.83$. Those shifts are directionally meaningful, but still small relative to the vintage gap itself.

The Fukushima shock therefore clarifies the argument rather than overturning it. Even a large change in energy-market conditions did not erase the cross-facility hierarchy or generate broad conversion among the non-generating majority. The fleet responded, but within a design envelope. The sign instability of the grid-emission control reinforces the point that broader time trends moved with the fleet rather than revealing a stable causal relationship, which is why that variable is retained only as a control.

\subsection{Net-Zero Implications: A Heterogeneous Transition}

Taken together, the results describe a fleet that is modernizing unevenly rather than converging smoothly. Gross avoided emissions from waste-to-energy reached about 4.6 million t-CO$_2$ in FY2024, but that contribution is concentrated in a relatively small modern segment and coexists with a majority of facilities that still generate no electricity. The net-zero problem is therefore not simply to raise average efficiency, but to manage a split fleet in which a high-performing group already exists alongside an ageing non-generating majority. In that sense, the fleet now resembles a modernizing vanguard layered on top of a much slower-moving remainder.

That split implies a policy hierarchy. For the modern generating segment, the main tasks are to preserve utilization, maintain reliable operation, and route waste toward the best-performing facilities. For the weakest segment, especially old non-generators and small plants, the relevant question is not how to squeeze out large operational gains but how to plan retirement, replacement, or major refurbishment without disrupting waste-treatment capacity. The pathway audit reinforces this distinction by showing more reset- or rebuild-like observed transitions than continuity-type upgrades in the coded panel.

The climate case for modernization is also time-sensitive. The avoided-emissions value of waste-to-energy electricity depends on the carbon intensity of the grid it displaces, so the payoff from moving old facilities into more efficient capital configurations is larger earlier in the decarbonization path than later. That does not make modernization optional; it strengthens the case for acting before the grid itself becomes much cleaner.

Within the circular-economy hierarchy, this thesis addresses only the residual-waste tier. It does not argue that energy recovery should outrank waste reduction or recycling. It argues that, conditional on continued reliance on incineration, Japan's climate-compatible path runs through a smaller, younger, better-utilized generating fleet rather than through diffuse late-life improvement across the existing old-small segment.

\subsection{Limitations}

\subsubsection{Data Limitations}

The analysis depends on the accuracy and completeness of the General Waste Treatment Survey, which is an administrative dataset subject to the reporting conventions and potential errors of prefectural and municipal administrators. While the survey is near-census in coverage, systematic reporting biases, for example, if municipalities with low-performing facilities are more likely to under-report efficiency metrics, could bias the regression estimates. The analysis can partially address this concern through the robustness checks, but cannot fully rule it out without access to independently verified facility-level data.

Heating value is an estimated rather than measured variable, as described in Section 3.3.3. The null result on heating value may partly reflect measurement error rather than (or in addition to) a genuine absence of effect. More precise waste composition monitoring would allow this question to be revisited.

\subsubsection{Causal Identification}

The regression analysis identifies conditional correlations between facility characteristics and efficiency, associations that are stable across model specifications, consistent with theoretical priors, and robust to multiple specification choices. However, causal identification in the strict econometric sense requires either random variation in treatment (which does not exist for facility age or design capacity) or a valid instrumental variable (which this thesis does not employ).

The age coefficient, in particular, should be interpreted as reflecting the combined effect of design vintage and physical ageing, two mechanisms that are observationally equivalent in this data. The within-facility variation analysis provides indirect descriptive evidence that vintage rather than degradation is the dominant mechanism, and that within-facility responsiveness is bounded in the canonical generator sample, but it does not by itself uniquely identify physical lock-in relative to retrofit history, governance constraints, or other omitted channels. Direct identification of the vintage effect would require data on facility design specifications at construction, data that are not available in the General Waste Treatment Survey.

\subsubsection{External Validity}

The findings pertain specifically to Japan's municipal waste incineration fleet, which has characteristics that may not generalize to WtE contexts in other countries: predominantly stoker furnace technology, municipal ownership, very limited district heating integration, and heterogeneous waste composition. The scale of the age effect, in particular, may differ in contexts where facility design has evolved differently or where maintenance regimes are more intensive.

Within Japan, the findings are based on fiscal years 2005--2024 and reflect the conditions of that period. Future changes in waste composition (particularly changes in plastic content following recent packaging regulations), energy market structure (as renewable energy penetration increases and the grid emission factor continues to decline), or facility technology (if new combustion or gasification technologies gain market share) could alter the efficiency relationships estimated here.

\section{Conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

This thesis asked a two-part question: which facilities record transition into power generation, and conditional on generation, which facilities achieve high energy recovery efficiency? Using 23,599 facility-year observations from FY2005--FY2024, the evidence supports a two-part answer. Transition into generation is selective on the extensive margin, while performance within the generating segment is strongly shaped by age, scale, and utilization.

On the extensive margin, 141 observed first-adoption events occur among 2,035 facilities first observed without power generation, and those events are concentrated among younger and larger plants. Facilities older than 10 years in the prior observed year are roughly 1.1--1.8 percentage points less likely to record transition in the current year, while each additional 100 t/day of prior-year capacity raises annual transition probability by about 0.5 percentage points. The pathway audit shows more reset- or rebuild-like transitions than continuity-type upgrades, but does not uniquely separate replacement, major refurbishment, and new build.

On the intensive margin, generator efficiency is consistently lower at older facilities and higher at larger and more fully utilized ones. Mean efficiency declines from 0.400 MWh/t among facilities aged 0--10 years to 0.183 MWh/t among facilities over 30 years old, and the within-to-total variance ratio of 0.1499 shows that cross-facility differences dominate within-facility movement over time. Together with the bounded Fukushima response, that pattern supports an interpretation of bounded responsiveness within the generator sample rather than frequent large late-life reversals.

At fleet level, Japan's incineration sector has modernized, but unevenly. Facility count fell from 1,318 to 1,014 between 2005 and 2024, the power-generating share rose from 21.6\% to 41.1\%, and gross avoided emissions from waste-to-energy reached about 4.6 million t-CO$_2$ in FY2024. Yet 59\% of active facilities still generated no electricity. The main conclusion is therefore not that the transition has failed, but that it remains split between a modernizing generating segment and an ageing non-generating remainder.

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

This thesis makes two contributions to the industrial ecology literature. First, it shows that Japan's incineration transition is empirically two-part: adoption into generation is an extensive-margin modernization problem, while efficiency among generators is a separate intensive-margin performance problem. That distinction is useful beyond this case because infrastructure transitions often blur adoption and conditional performance into one narrative.

Second, it provides facility-level evidence consistent with bounded infrastructural lock-in within the generating segment. The low within-facility efficiency variation documented here, remaining low across the pre- and post-Fukushima subsamples, answers \citet{Seto2016}'s call for facility-level tests in a qualified but important way. Performance responds to incentives, but within an envelope set largely by vintage and design. In that sense, the thesis extends the lock-in literature with a waste-sector case that is convergent with recent facility-level evidence from China \citep{Cui2026}.

\subsection{Policy Recommendations}

The policy recommendations below are inferential implications of the two-part evidence, not directly estimated governance effects.

\textbf{First, accelerate retirement and replacement planning for the old non-generating segment.} The results are more consistent with a capital-planning strategy than with diffuse late-life conversion of small ageing plants. The weakest segment should therefore be treated primarily as a retirement and major-renewal problem tied to regional waste-routing plans.

\textbf{Second, move consolidation planning above the single-municipality level.} The positive capacity effect means that scale gains are real, but current municipal fragmentation prevents them from being captured quickly. Prefecture-level or multi-municipal planning is a plausible institutional response if policymakers want the scale premium to reach a larger share of the fleet.

\textbf{Third, use monitoring and finance to support fleet transition rather than expecting operations alone to close vintage gaps.} Annual performance data appear more valuable as a fleet vintage register and prioritization tool than as a stand-alone operational remedy. Capital support should therefore emphasize high-efficiency renewal and documented major upgrading, while still preserving routing and utilization improvements within the already-generating segment.

\subsection{Directions for Future Research}

Three extensions would most strengthen this research agenda. First, case-based work on successful and failed regional consolidation would complement the statistical results with institutional detail. Second, retrofit histories and major-upgrade records would allow a cleaner separation of vintage effects from refurbishment effects inside the current ``reset'' and ``continuity'' categories. Third, in longer-horizon follow-on work, adding facility-level heat recovery and future grid-decarbonization scenarios would clarify how the climate value of waste-to-energy changes as Japan's power system becomes cleaner.

\subsection{Closing Statement}

Japan built an incineration fleet for an era when burning waste was the best available option; the question its net-zero commitment now poses is not whether to change that fleet, but how quickly the country can replace what it has already locked in.
