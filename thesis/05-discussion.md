# Chapter 5: Discussion

## 5.1 Overview

The regression results presented in Chapter 4 are internally consistent across eight model specifications and align with the theoretical predictions of the industrial ecology framework introduced in Chapter 2. This chapter interprets those results for their theoretical and policy significance. Section 5.2 discusses the core finding — that efficiency is design-determined — and its implications for lock-in theory. Section 5.3 examines the scale and utilization findings through the lens of fleet consolidation policy. Section 5.4 considers the Fukushima shock and what it reveals about the responsiveness of the fleet to energy market signals. Section 5.5 addresses the implications for Japan's net-zero trajectory. Section 5.6 discusses the limitations of the analysis.

## 5.2 The Design-Determination Finding: Empirical Lock-In

The single most consequential finding of this thesis is not a regression coefficient — it is the within-facility variation ratio of approximately 0.11 for log-efficiency. This number means that roughly 89% of the variation in energy recovery efficiency across the Japanese incineration fleet is between facilities (reflecting design vintage, scale, and location) rather than within facilities over time (reflecting operational improvement or degradation). This ratio is stable across the pre-Fukushima (0.10) and post-Fukushima (0.10) subsamples, confirming it is a structural property of the fleet rather than a period-specific artifact. Facilities that were efficient in 2005 remain efficient in 2024; facilities that were inefficient in 2005 remain inefficient.

This finding constitutes unusually clean empirical evidence for the lock-in mechanism as theorized by Unruh [-@Unruh2000]. Lock-in theory predicts that infrastructure systems exhibit low within-facility variation in performance over time, because physical capital embeds past design decisions that cannot be undone without replacement. The prediction is confirmed here: approximately 89% of variance is between-facility, a ratio that is consistent across subsamples and time periods. In a dataset spanning 20 years and covering nearly a thousand facilities, the absence of within-facility efficiency trends is a systematic, not accidental, property of the data.

The policy implication is direct and uncomfortable: operational optimization cannot solve the efficiency problem. Japan has a large segment of its incineration fleet — particularly the 30% of power-generating facilities that are more than 25 years old — whose efficiency is essentially fixed at the design level of 1990s or early 2000s engineering. No combination of feed-in tariff adjustments, operator incentives, or operational guidance will meaningfully improve the performance of these facilities. The efficiency gap between old and new facilities (0.397 MWh/t versus 0.179 MWh/t for the youngest and oldest cohorts respectively) is not a gap that can be closed by better management. It is a design gap that can only be addressed by replacing old facilities with new ones.

This conclusion aligns with the "stranded asset" framing from the sustainability transitions literature [@Caldecott2016]. Facilities that are 25–35 years old face a choice: expensive retrofit or planned retirement. The regression evidence that age effects are continuous and stable — not concentrated in particular age ranges where degradation accelerates — suggests that the engineering constraint is architectural rather than component-specific. This is consistent with the engineering literature on WtE facility design, which emphasises that boiler pressure ratings, turbine specifications, and waste handling systems are integral to the structural design of the building and cannot be upgraded independently without prohibitive cost [@Niessen2010]. While targeted retrofits (e.g., turbine upgrades or improved combustion controls) may yield incremental improvements, the within-facility variation ratio of 0.11 suggests that such improvements have not materially altered the efficiency trajectory of existing facilities over the 20-year study period.

### 5.2.1 What "Design-Determined" Means in Practice

The finding that efficiency is design-determined has a practical corollary that deserves emphasis: measurement and monitoring of within-facility efficiency over time is, for the purposes of *predicting future performance*, essentially redundant. If a facility's efficiency in year 1 of operation strongly predicts its efficiency in year 15 of operation, then the expensive apparatus of annual efficiency auditing and reporting provides little information for performance management purposes.

This does not mean that monitoring is useless — it may serve accountability, billing, and grid management purposes — but it does suggest that the monitoring data could be used more productively to inform fleet-level planning (which facilities to retire, which to replace with what capacity) rather than facility-level operational guidance (how should each facility adjust its operations this year). The Ministry of the Environment's General Waste Treatment Survey, which forms the data basis for this thesis, is an asset for fleet-level strategic planning that appears underutilized for this purpose.

## 5.3 Scale, Consolidation, and the Governance Challenge

The positive effect of design capacity on efficiency — approximately 0.09–0.10 log-units per 100 t/day, consistent across all specifications — provides an economic justification for the consolidation trend already underway in Japan's fleet. If two 100 t/day facilities were replaced by one 200 t/day facility processing the same total waste volume, the regression estimates predict approximately 9–10% higher efficiency from the scale gain, on top of any efficiency improvement from newer design vintage.

In reality, the consolidation opportunity is even larger because small facilities are systematically older (the correlation between facility size and age in the dataset is negative — smaller facilities tend to be older, reflecting the general tendency toward larger facilities in more recent construction), meaning that replacing small old facilities with large new ones simultaneously captures both the scale premium and the vintage premium. If a 1985-vintage 80 t/day facility (predicted efficiency: roughly 0.15–0.18 MWh/t) were replaced by a 2024-vintage 200 t/day facility (predicted efficiency: roughly 0.38–0.42 MWh/t), the efficiency improvement would be approximately twofold. This is the arithmetic of Japan's consolidation strategy, and the data confirm that it is working: mean fleet efficiency among power-generating facilities has risen from 0.243 to 0.298 MWh/t over the study period, driven by exactly this replacement dynamic.

The challenge is governance, not engineering. Japan's waste management system is organized at the municipal level, with individual municipalities responsible for contracting, financing, and operating their own facilities. Municipal boundaries frequently do not align with the catchment areas that would optimize waste routing for efficiency purposes. A regional facility serving five municipalities is more efficient than five municipal facilities of equivalent combined capacity, but building it requires five municipalities to agree on a governance structure for joint operation, cost-sharing, and decision-making authority — an institutional challenge that has proven difficult in many contexts [@TabataTsai2016].

The national government has created financial incentives for inter-municipal cooperation in waste management, including grants for joint facility construction and technical assistance from the Ministry of the Environment [@MOEJ2024]. The data show that these incentives have contributed to the consolidation trend — facility count has declined consistently — but the pace is slow. At the rate of approximately 15 facility closures per year, reducing the fleet from its current 1,014 to a hypothetical target of 600 large modern facilities (a rough estimate based on the average capacity of facilities built after 2010 applied to Japan's total waste throughput) would take another 27 years — well past Japan's 2050 net-zero deadline.

### 5.3.1 The Utilization Dimension

The strong positive effect of capacity utilization on efficiency (+0.58 to +0.62 in the main specifications) opens a second, complementary policy lever: improving utilization at existing high-efficiency facilities rather than building new ones. If waste routing could be reorganized to send more waste to modern high-capacity facilities — which typically have excess capacity — and less to small old facilities that may be near or above their design throughput, average fleet efficiency would improve without any new construction.

This "routing optimization" approach is appealing because it does not require the large capital investment or long lead times of facility replacement. However, it faces similar governance obstacles: municipalities that own or contract for their own facilities are reluctant to route waste to a neighboring municipality's facility, even if that facility is more efficient, because doing so undermines the fiscal case for maintaining their own infrastructure.

The utilization finding also has a dynamic interpretation. As the fleet consolidates and waste volumes are concentrated in fewer facilities, average utilization should increase — which the model predicts will further improve efficiency. This is a positive feedback loop: consolidation raises utilization, which raises efficiency, which makes the case for further consolidation. The data are consistent with this dynamic: facilities in the most recent years of the sample show modestly higher utilization rates than in earlier years, consistent with increasing concentration of waste flows.

## 5.4 The Fukushima Effect: Energy Markets and Fleet Responsiveness

The robustness checks comparing pre- and post-Fukushima periods (R1–R4 in Table 4.5) reveal two patterns that illuminate how energy market conditions interact with the structural determinants of efficiency.

First, the age coefficient is slightly smaller in magnitude in the post-Fukushima period (approximately −0.036 vs. −0.041). One interpretation is that the Fukushima shock, by dramatically increasing the value of grid electricity and providing policy incentives for WtE generation through the 2012 FiT, created circumstances in which some older facilities invested in efficiency improvements they might not otherwise have made. Even a modest maintenance investment in boiler tubes, turbine components, or steam system insulation can partially offset the vintage disadvantage of older facilities. Under normal energy market conditions, these investments have marginal value; under the post-Fukushima premium electricity pricing, they became worthwhile. If this interpretation is correct, the age coefficient represents a lower bound on the vintage effect — one that is partially compressed by the unusual incentive environment of the post-Fukushima period.

Second, the utilization coefficient is larger in the post-Fukushima period (+0.63 vs. +0.60). This is consistent with facilities operating more intensively to maximize electricity generation when the value of that generation increased. The Fukushima shock effectively raised the marginal return to utilization, inducing facilities to process waste more continuously and reduce downtime for maintenance.

The instability of the grid emission factor coefficient across specifications — positive in pooled OLS, negative with year fixed effects — should also be understood in this context. The grid emission factor in Japan rose sharply after Fukushima (as nuclear generation was replaced by fossil fuel generation), then gradually declined as renewables expanded. This time pattern is correlated with a period during which fleet efficiency was also rising (because newer, more efficient facilities were coming online). In the pooled OLS specification, the emission factor appears positively correlated with efficiency because both are trending together. With year fixed effects, this common trend is absorbed, and the remaining variation in emission factors is cross-regional rather than over-time — a variation that has a less clear relationship with facility-level efficiency decisions.

The Fukushima event is thus a natural experiment that reveals the limited responsiveness of the fleet's efficiency distribution to energy market signals. Even a shock of this magnitude — eliminating approximately 30% of Japan's generating capacity virtually overnight and driving dramatic changes in electricity prices and policy — produced only modest changes in the coefficients that describe the efficiency gradient. The fundamental structure of the fleet — predominantly old, small, and (for the majority) non-power-generating — was not transformed by Fukushima. What the shock did was accelerate the modernization trend already underway and generate additional policy support for the consolidation agenda. But the lock-in was not broken.

## 5.5 Net-Zero Implications: A Heterogeneous Transition

The findings of this thesis, taken together, paint a picture of a fleet in the early stages of circular transition rather than in a state of completed modernization. The gross avoided emissions of approximately 4.6 million t-CO₂ in FY2024 (an upper bound, as process emissions from combustion are not deducted) represent a meaningful contribution — approximately 0.4% of Japan's total annual CO₂ emissions of approximately 1.07 billion t-CO₂ [@MOEJ2024] — but it is highly concentrated in a small number of modern, large-scale facilities and is achieved despite the fact that 59% of the fleet generates no electricity at all.

The net-zero trajectory for the waste sector requires thinking about two distinct populations within the fleet.

**The modernizing vanguard** — approximately 200 facilities built after 2000, with design capacities exceeding 200 t/day — already perform at international best-practice levels of efficiency. These facilities are not a policy problem; they are a policy asset. Ensuring their continued operation, maintaining their utilization at high levels, and where possible expanding their capacity through incremental upgrades should be priorities. The evidence from the post-Fukushima period suggests that energy market signals can improve utilization at these facilities, making revenue certainty through long-term power purchase agreements or expanded FiT coverage an appropriate supporting policy.

**The ageing majority** — the 598 non-power-generating facilities and the approximately 200 power-generating facilities built before 1995 — represents the carbon lock-in challenge. For these facilities, the appropriate policy question is not "how do we improve their efficiency?" but "how do we manage their retirement in a way that minimizes waste management disruption and maximizes the opportunity for replacement with modern infrastructure?"

This framing has a direct implication for Japan's waste management planning cycle. Municipalities currently make facility renewal decisions on a case-by-case basis, often within a horizon of 10–15 years, which is inadequate for the kind of coordinated regional planning that would allow the consolidation of waste flows and the construction of replacement facilities at optimal scale. A national-level facility vintage register — mapping the age distribution of operating facilities and projecting retirement timelines — would enable the Ministry of the Environment to coordinate regional renewal planning in a way that is currently impossible.

### 5.5.1 The 59% Problem

The persistence of non-power-generating facilities deserves specific attention. These 598 facilities represent a substantial share of Japan's incineration capacity and process a significant portion of the country's municipal waste. They burn that waste and release the thermal energy to the atmosphere — a waste of energy resource that has been characterized in the European WtE literature as "landfilling in the air" [@EuropeanCommission2017].

The barriers to adding power generation at existing non-generating facilities are both technical and economic. Technically, many small and old non-generating facilities lack the boiler and turbine infrastructure required for electricity generation; adding this equipment requires major civil works that may approach or exceed the cost of new construction. Economically, the scale of waste processing at small facilities may be insufficient to justify the capital investment in power generation infrastructure, even at current electricity prices. For facilities processing fewer than 50–70 t/day, the economics of power generation are generally unfavorable regardless of facility age.

The policy implication is that the non-generating segment cannot be converted to generating in place — it must be replaced. And because replacement at the facility level without regional consolidation simply perpetuates the fragmented structure that limits scale economies, the replacement strategy must be coordinated at the regional level. This is, again, a governance challenge more than a technical one.

### 5.5.2 Waste-to-Energy in the Circular Economy Hierarchy

A legitimate question from the circular economy perspective is whether improving WtE efficiency is the right goal, or whether policy energy should instead be directed toward reducing the waste streams that require incineration in the first place. This thesis does not take a position on the relative priority of waste reduction, reuse, recycling, and energy recovery — that is a broader question about Japan's material economy that extends well beyond the scope of this analysis. What the thesis does contribute is a clearer picture of the energy recovery tier as it currently exists: highly heterogeneous, partially modernized, and constrained by design decisions made decades ago.

Within the tier of residual waste that requires thermal treatment — waste that cannot be economically recycled and would otherwise require landfilling — the evidence is clear that modern, large-scale WtE is substantially more efficient than old, small-scale incineration. To the extent that Japan will continue to manage residual waste through incineration (and all demographic and policy projections suggest it will, for the foreseeable future), the findings of this thesis are unambiguous about the direction of improvement: larger, newer, and more fully utilized.

## 5.6 Limitations

### 5.6.1 Data Limitations

The analysis depends on the accuracy and completeness of the General Waste Treatment Survey, which is an administrative dataset subject to the reporting conventions and potential errors of prefectural and municipal administrators. While the survey is near-census in coverage, systematic reporting biases — for example, if municipalities with low-performing facilities are more likely to under-report efficiency metrics — could bias the regression estimates. The analysis can partially address this concern through the robustness checks, but cannot fully rule it out without access to independently verified facility-level data.

Heating value is an estimated rather than measured variable, as described in Section 3.3.3. The null result on heating value may partly reflect measurement error rather than (or in addition to) a genuine absence of effect. More precise waste composition monitoring would allow this question to be revisited.

### 5.6.2 Causal Identification

The regression analysis identifies conditional correlations between facility characteristics and efficiency — associations that are stable across model specifications, consistent with theoretical priors, and robust to multiple specification choices. However, causal identification in the strict econometric sense requires either random variation in treatment (which does not exist for facility age or design capacity) or a valid instrumental variable (which this thesis does not employ).

The age coefficient, in particular, should be interpreted as reflecting the combined effect of design vintage and physical aging, two mechanisms that are observationally equivalent in this data. The within-facility variation analysis provides indirect evidence that vintage rather than degradation is the dominant mechanism, but direct identification of the vintage effect would require data on facility design specifications at construction — data that are not available in the General Waste Treatment Survey.

### 5.6.3 External Validity

The findings pertain specifically to Japan's municipal waste incineration fleet, which has characteristics — predominantly stoker furnace technology, municipal ownership, no district heating, heterogeneous waste composition — that may not generalize to WtE contexts in other countries. The scale of the age effect, in particular, may differ in contexts where facility design has evolved differently or where maintenance regimes are more intensive.

Within Japan, the findings are based on fiscal years 2005–2024 and reflect the conditions of that period. Future changes in waste composition (particularly changes in plastic content following recent packaging regulations), energy market structure (as renewable energy penetration increases and the grid emission factor continues to decline), or facility technology (if new combustion or gasification technologies gain market share) could alter the efficiency relationships estimated here.
